\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Research Background}
Open-source software (OSS) ecosystems have become an indispensable digital foundation for modern society. A vast majority of software development, corporate operations, and academic research worldwide depend directly or indirectly on these OSS projects.

However, existing literature reports that many OSS projects rely heavily on the dedicated contributions of a small number of ``core developers''\cite{sample}. Core developers are defined as a key group of contributors who possess deep technical knowledge of the project and hold the authority to make significant changes to the source code. Even in large-scale projects, the developers responsible for the most critical parts of the codebase are often extremely limited in number\cite{sample}. This structure entails an inherent vulnerability: the departure or burnout of these core developers can lead directly to the stagnation or termination of the project.

This structural vulnerability gained significant public attention following severe security incidents such as Heartbleed (OpenSSL)\cite{sample} and Log4Shell (Log4j)\cite{sample}. The fact that software underpinning global infrastructure is maintained by a handful of volunteers has highlighted a critical lack of sustainability in the modern digital ecosystem\cite{sample}.

\subsection{Motivation and Problem Statement}
To address these sustainability challenges, funding platforms such as Open Collective\cite{sample} and GitHub Sponsors\cite{sample} have emerged, allowing projects to solicit financial support directly. These platforms provide a mechanism to inject external capital into activities that were previously performed on a purely volunteer basis, thereby supporting stable project management.

In theory, OSS projects are maintained by voluntary contributions. However, if these projects increasingly depend on external funding for continuous maintenance and development, the cessation of such funds could jeopardize the longevity of the projects and, by extension, the health of the entire ecosystem that relies on them.

While many projects are now receiving significant financial contributions, the specific impact of these funds on actual software development activities remains poorly understood. Specifically, the correlation and causal relationships between funding and development productivity have not yet been sufficiently quantified.

\subsection{Research Objectives}
The objective of this research is to investigate the relationship between external funding and software development activities in OSS projects based on empirical data. Specifically, we address the following three Research Questions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{RQ1: Investigation of Financial Contributions.} Why are they funding these open-source projects? How much are they donating?
    \item \textbf{RQ2: Analysis of Funding Expenditures.} For what purposes (e.g., engineering, marketing, or operations) do projects spend the funds they receive?
    \item \textbf{RQ3: Quantitative Measurement of Impact on Development.} How do the acquisition and expenditure of funds correlate with GitHub development activities, such as the number of pull requests, commits, and active developers?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Methodology Overview}
In this study, we utilize the Open Collective API\cite{sample} to collect the complete transaction histories of target OSS projects. This dataset includes granular metadata such as expenditure purposes, transaction amounts, and timestamps.

Next, we correlate this financial data with GitHub contribution histories to analyze changes in development activity before and after the acquisition of funds or specific expenditures (e.g., payments for engineering tasks). Through this approach, we verify whether the injection of capital directly contributes to more active maintenance and development.

\subsection{Significance of the Research}
The projects targeted in this study are critical OSS infrastructures used by companies, organizations, and researchers worldwide. If it is revealed that these projects rely heavily on donations for their maintenance, it will provide crucial insights into the potential risks and vulnerabilities of the global software ecosystem.

The findings of this research will provide essential guidelines for developer communities seeking sustainability, companies dependent on OSS, and funding platform operators to evaluate the effectiveness of financial support and the overall health of projects.

\section{Background and Related Work}

\subsection{OSS Sustainability and Core Developers}

The sustainability of open-source software (OSS) is a critical concern as modern society increasingly relies on these projects as a digital foundation\cite{sample}. However, research has shown that many OSS projects suffer from a high concentration of knowledge, relying on a very small group of core developers. 

To quantify this risk, the ``Truck Factor'' metric\cite{sample} was proposed to represent the minimum number of developers who must leave a project before it becomes incapacitated\cite{sample}. Empirical studies on popular GitHub repositories have revealed that 65\% of these projects have a Truck Factor of two or less\cite{sample}. Furthermore, a large-scale study of over 36,000 projects found that 89\% of projects have experienced the loss of their core development team at least once, with 70\% of these abandonments occurring within the first three years of the project's life\cite{sample}. Only 27\% of projects that lose their core team are able to survive and attract new core developers\cite{sample}.

The danger of this structural vulnerability is well illustrated by high-profile security incidents. The Log4Shell vulnerability in the Log4j library exposed how critical global infrastructure was maintained by uncompensated volunteers, some of whom worked 22-hour shifts to mitigate the crisis\cite{sample}. Similarly, the Heartbleed incident in OpenSSL revealed that a project securing the majority of the internet was operating on an annual donation budget of only US\$2,000, with only one full-time developer\cite{sample}. These cases highlight the urgent need for external funding to support core developers and ensure long-term sustainability\cite{sample}.

\subsection{Funding in Open-source Ecosystems}
The emergence of platforms like Open Collective has introduced unprecedented transparency to the financial management of open-source projects by making all transactions publicly visible. While corporate entities tend to provide larger individual donations, individual contributors are essential for long-term sustainability\cite{sample}, as they often provide higher total funding and demonstrate greater stickiness through recurring support\cite{sample}. However, projects like OpenSSL illustrate that reliance on grassroots funding alone can be insufficient for critical infrastructure, often resulting in chronic underfunding until a major crisis occurs.

Although a positive correlation has been observed between corporate donation levels and project maintenance activity, the specific impact of diverse expenditure types on development productivity remains poorly understood. This study addresses this research gap by quantitatively analyzing the relationship between these financial flows and GitHub development metrics.

\subsection{Empirical Studies on Development Activity}
To evaluate the impact of external events on OSS projects, researchers typically utilize established repository metrics. Indicators such as the number of commits and pull requests (PRs) are widely accepted as proxies for development activity and productivity. Additionally, community metrics such as stars and forks are used to measure project popularity and visibility.

Studies focusing on social media exposure, such as promotion on Hacker News, have demonstrated that increased visibility can lead to a significant spike in forks, stars, and the number of contributors shortly after the project is mentioned\cite{sample}. Research has also indicated that projects receiving positive community sentiment through these platforms exhibit higher growth in commits and PRs\cite{sample}. This study builds upon these established empirical methods to investigate whether the injection of financial resources through funding platforms leads to similar quantifiable improvements in development activity.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Data Collection}

\subsection{Labeling of Expense Categories}

To investigate how OSS projects use the funds they receive, we label each expense and analyze the labeled data. First, we define a set of high-level categories based on the purpose of the expense. Then, we classify individual expense records into these categories. For the classification step, we consider two approaches: a machine-learning-based method and an LLM-based method.

\subsubsection{Definition of Expense Categories}

We define eight high-level categories based on the purpose of each expense in Table~\ref{tab:expense-categories}.
Each category is designed to capture a distinct aspect of how OSS projects utilize their funds.

\begin{table}[htbp]
    \centering
    \caption{Definitions of Expense Categories}\label{tab:expense-categories}
    \begin{tabular}{lp{10cm}}
        \toprule
        Category & Description \\
        \midrule
        \textbf{Development} & Compensation paid to official project members for direct software development and maintenance. \\
        \textbf{Bounty} & Rewards or fees paid to external contributors (non-members) for specific tasks, bug fixes, or feature implementations. \\
        \textbf{Infra \& Subscription} & Recurring costs for cloud hosting, internet connectivity, and other software-as-a-service (SaaS) subscriptions. \\
        \textbf{Equipment} & Purchase of physical hardware and assets directly used for development activities, such as laptops and servers. \\
        \textbf{Food \& Supplies} & Purchase of consumables, meals, and general physical items that are not directly related to development. \\
        \textbf{Marketing \& Events} & Costs for organizing or participating in events to promote the project and recruit new developers (includes marketing, promotion, transportation, registration fees). \\
        \textbf{Non-tech Activities} & Essential tasks not directly linked to coding, such as documentation, translation, legal or tax compliance, and other miscellaneous fees. \\
        \textbf{Unknown} & Expenditures where the purpose cannot be determined at all due to missing or insufficient information. \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsubsection{Machine Learning-based Classification of Expenses}

First, we manually created high-level expense categories based on the tagsin Table~\ref{tab:expense-categories} assigned in \texttt{expense\_tags}.

Using this tag information, we assigned category labels to 7,003 expense records, which were used as the training dataset.

For the remaining records, we performed machine learning-based classification using the following transaction metadata as features:

\begin{itemize}
\item from\_account\_type
\item to\_account\_name
\item to\_account\_type
\item expense\_type
\item description
\item expense\_description
\end{itemize}

TF-IDF was used to extract textual features, and Logistic Regression was employed as the classifier.

\subsubsection{LLM-based Classification of Expenses}

We used a Large Language Model (LLM) to classify the expense data. The process followed these steps:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Manual Labeling}: First, we randomly picked 381 records from the entire dataset. Two authors manually labeled these records by reading the \texttt{expense\_description}.
    \item \textbf{Comparison and Agreement}: We compared the labels from the two authors. When our opinions were different, we discussed them to decide the final label. We did this carefully to make the labels as accurate as possible.
    \item \textbf{Prompt Tuning}: Next, we gave the same 381 descriptions to the LLM for labeling. We adjusted the prompt so that the LLM's results would match our manual labels as much as possible.
    \item \textbf{Full Classification}: Finally, we used the LLM with the adjusted prompt to classify all the remaining data.
\end{enumerate}

We categorized how collectives utilize the funds they receive.

\subsubsection{Expense Classification by LLM}

\subsubsection{Method}

To analyze how funds are utilized in OSS projects, we classify expense descriptions into predefined categories using a large language model (LLM).

We define 11 expense categories, including \textit{Development}, \textit{Bounty}, \textit{Infra-subscription}, and \textit{Miscellaneous}, each with a detailed textual definition (Table~\ref{tab:expense-categories}).

To evaluate the reliability of the LLM-based classification, we construct a manually labeled dataset.

From the full dataset, we randomly sample 381 expense records. Each record is carefully annotated by the author and a supervising professor to ensure high-quality labels.

The annotated dataset serves as ground truth for evaluating the classification performance of the LLM.

\subsubsection{Evaluation}

We compare the predicted labels generated by the LLM with the manually assigned ground truth labels.

We report standard classification metrics, including accuracy and macro-averaged F1 score, as well as per-class precision, recall, and F1 scores.

The results are as follows:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Accuracy: 0.78
    \item Macro F1-score: 0.76
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Findings}

The results reveal a clear pattern in the performance of the LLM-based classification.

First, categories with well-defined and concrete semantics, such as \textit{Development}, \textit{Infra-subscription}, and \textit{Travel}, achieve high performance, with F1-scores exceeding 0.80. This indicates that the LLM is effective at identifying expenses with explicit and unambiguous purposes.

In contrast, categories with inherently ambiguous or overlapping definitions, such as \textit{Non-tech Service}, \textit{Miscellaneous}, and \textit{Unknown}, show significantly lower performance. In particular, \textit{Non-tech Service} achieves an F1-score of only 0.21, suggesting that distinguishing non-coding contributions from development-related activities is challenging based solely on textual descriptions.

These results suggest that the difficulty of classification is strongly influenced by the clarity and separability of category definitions. Even human annotators may struggle with such distinctions, which limits the achievable performance of automated methods.

Overall, while the LLM demonstrates strong performance on clearly defined expense types, its effectiveness is limited for ambiguous or weakly defined categories.
